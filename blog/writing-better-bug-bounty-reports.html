Writing Bug Bounty Reports That Actually Get Triaged (and Paid)

The bug finds the vulnerability. The report decides whether it gets paid, and how fast.

Heads up: Educational content only. This post is about report writing practice, not a specific vulnerability.
TL;DR Lead with impact, give exact reproduction steps a stranger could follow without asking a single question, attach proof, and suggest a fix. Everything else is optional padding.

I've had technically identical bugs get triaged in an hour and get stuck in "needs more info" for two weeks, and the only difference was the report. Triagers read a lot of submissions. A report that respects their time gets prioritized, and getting prioritized is most of the battle.

The structure I use every time

  1. Title — specific enough to be searchable later: "IDOR on /api/v1/invoices/{id} allows reading any user's invoice PDF," not "IDOR vulnerability found"
  2. Summary — two or three sentences: what the bug is, where it is, and what an attacker gains
  3. Severity / impact — my own CVSS estimate or the program's severity rubric, plus a plain-English sentence on real-world impact
  4. Steps to reproduce — numbered, exact, no assumed context
  5. Proof of concept — request/response pairs, a screenshot, or a short screen recording
  6. Suggested fix — even a one-liner shows you understand the root cause, not just the symptom

Steps to reproduce: assume nothing

The single biggest source of triage delay is a report that only makes sense to the person who wrote it. Compare:

Bad:
"Change the invoice ID in the URL and you can see other people's invoices."

Good:
1. Log in as Account A (testuser1@example.com).
2. Navigate to Billing > Invoices, open any invoice, note the URL:
   https://app.target.com/invoices/48213
3. Change 48213 to 48214 (an invoice belonging to Account B) and reload.
4. Observe the page loads Account B's invoice, including full name,
   address, and itemized charges, while still authenticated as Account A.

A triager should be able to follow your steps without opening a second tab to ask you a clarifying question. Every clarifying question is a round trip that can take days depending on the program's response time.

Proof beats prose

A raw HTTP request and response, or a 20-second screen recording, resolves ambiguity faster than any amount of written explanation. I include both whenever the bug allows it:

GET /invoices/48214 HTTP/1.1
Host: app.target.com
Cookie: session=<account-A-session>

HTTP/1.1 200 OK
{"id":48214,"owner":"account-B","total":"389.50", ...}

Redact session tokens and personal data belonging to real accounts before submitting anything, and use your own test accounts wherever possible instead of real user data.

State the impact like you mean it, but don't oversell

"This could potentially maybe lead to..." undersells a bug you've already proven. "This allows any authenticated user to read every other user's invoices, including full billing addresses" states exactly what you demonstrated. At the same time, don't inflate a low-severity finding by chaining hypotheticals that you haven't actually shown — triagers see through that quickly, and it costs credibility on your next report too.

Handling "Not Applicable" and duplicates gracefully

Search existing reports before submitting when the program allows it, since duplicates simply don't pay regardless of how good the write-up is. When a report does come back N/A or informative and you genuinely disagree, a short, specific, evidence-based rebuttal is worth sending once. A second or third attempt to argue the same point rarely changes the outcome and does cost goodwill with the team on future reports.

Response time matters more than people think

When a triager asks a follow-up question, answering within a few hours instead of a few days keeps your report near the top of their queue. Reports that go quiet for a week on the researcher's side often get deprioritized simply because the triager moved on to newer submissions.

Key takeaways
  • Lead with a specific title and a short, clear impact summary
  • Write reproduction steps for a stranger with zero context
  • Attach proof — request/response pairs or a short recording
  • State impact accurately; don't undersell or oversell it
  • Respond fast to triager questions — it affects prioritization more than people expect
